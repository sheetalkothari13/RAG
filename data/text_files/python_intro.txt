    Concept:
Data is raw, unprocessed facts — numbers, characters, or signals — that by themselves carry no context (e.g.,
72, 'Ravi', 08). Information is data that has been processed, organized, or structured so it becomes meaningful
and useful for decision-making (e.g., 'Ravi scored 72 marks on 08-06-2026'). The transformation of data into
information happens through processing: sorting, classifying, summarizing, and calculating. Key properties of
good information are accuracy, timeliness, completeness, relevance, and reliability. In the
data-information-knowledge-wisdom (DIKW) hierarchy, data sits at the base, information is data with context,
knowledge is information with understanding of patterns, and wisdom is knowledge applied with judgement.
CPGET frequently tests the distinction between data and information, examples of each, and characteristics of
quality information.

Practice Questions:
Q1. Which of the following is an example of 'information' rather than raw 'data'?
Solution: 'The average monthly rainfall in Hyderabad in June is 120 mm' is information because raw daily
rainfall readings have been processed (averaged) and given context. A single reading like '15 mm' alone is data.
Q2. List three properties that make information 'good quality' for decision-making.
Solution: Accuracy (free from errors), Timeliness (available when needed), and Relevance (applicable to the
decision at hand). Completeness and reliability are also acceptable answers.
Q3. Explain with one example how the same data can produce different information for different users.
Solution: Sales figures (data) of a shop can be processed into 'total monthly revenue' for an owner, or into
'best-selling product' for a marketing team — same raw data, different processing goals, different information.
